The First Flash opens the collection with the supplication of the Prophet Jonah
(*Yunus*) — "There is no god but You; glory be to You! Indeed I was among the
wrongdoers" (21:87) — and asks what made so short a cry so powerful. Nursî sets
the scene: cast into a stormy sea, swallowed by a whale, in a dark and turbulent
night with every ordinary hope exhausted. To be saved, Jonah needed someone
whose single command could govern the night, the sea, and the whale all at once
— the three of them ranged against him. No created cause could help, for, in
Nursî's recurring conviction, **secondary causes** (*esbâb*) have no power of
their own. Seeing with certainty that there was no refuge but the *Causer of
Causes*, Jonah turned to Him in the pure light of divine unity (*tevhîd*), and
the whale's belly became, as it were, a submarine and the raging sea a quiet
plain.

Then comes the turn that makes the Flash ours. Our situation, Nursî argues, is a
hundred times more fearful than Jonah's. **Our night is the future** — darker
still when we look at it with heedless eyes. **Our sea is this spinning globe**,
every wave bearing away the dead. **Our whale is the caprice of our own soul**
(*nefis*), which gnaws not at a hundred-year life but at an eternal one. The
remedy is therefore exactly the same: turn your gaze away from the crowd of
causes and take refuge directly in the Lord who holds the future in His command,
the world in His jurisdiction, and the soul under His sway.

He then reads the prayer's three phrases as three appeals: with "there is no god
but You" we draw mercy over our future; with "glory be to You" over our world;
and with "I was among the wrongdoers" over our soul — so that belief (*îmân*)
lights the road ahead and turns dread into calm. In short: because a human being
fears the microbe and the falling star alike, and loves a small garden and an
endless Paradise alike, only the One in whose grasp are both atom and planet can
truly be his refuge — so we, too, should say what Jonah said.
