The Tenth Flash, "Compassionate Slaps," is the most personal piece so far: a
string of first-hand accounts gathered around a single verse, "Allah is Kind to
His servants" (3:30). Nursî first distinguishes three kinds of "wonder" that, he
believes, accompany the service of the Qur'an — one that prepares and urges the
workers on, one that removes obstacles and turns back opponents, and a third,
the lightest, which is his subject here: the gentle corrective he calls a
**compassionate slap** (*şefkat tokadı*).

The pattern is always the same. Whenever he himself, or one of his fellow
workers, grew lax in the service for some self-interested reason — to protect
his own comfort, his position, his safety — a misfortune followed that was the
exact reverse of the thing he had hoped to gain. Thinking only of saving himself,
Nursî is moved from one place of exile to another; a student who clings to his
fine house and homeland must leave both; another who hides his work to court an
official's favour is suddenly entangled in a lawsuit. He recounts some fourteen
such incidents, beginning with his own and including frank confessions from the
students themselves. Because the blow so precisely undoes the neglectful aim, he
reads these not as bad luck or as wrath, but as a tenderness (*şefkat*) that
wakes a slackening heart and calls it back to sincere (*ihlâs*) service —
exactly the lapse of heedlessness (*gaflet*) being corrected each time. He treats
the consistency of the pattern as a small sign, a wonder (*kerâmet*), attending
the work itself.

He closes with a question a reader will already be asking: why are his friends
slapped while his enemies go untouched? Because, he answers, a believer's fault
is a small wrongdoing that is mercifully and partly purified here and now, while
an opponent's unbelief is graver and deferred to the reckoning of the
hereafter — which is why "this world is the prison of the believer and the
paradise of the unbeliever."

Told in Nursî's own voice, with names and details, the accounts carry a candour
no summary conveys; the full text is linked above.
