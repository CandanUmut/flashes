The Eleventh Flash — "The Stairway of the Practices of the Prophet and Antidote
for the Sickness of Innovations" — sets out, in eleven points, why following the
Prophet's example (the *sünnet*) matters so much, especially in an age when it is
being let go.

Its opening points press the worth of the Sunna: a Hadith promises that whoever
holds to it when the community is corrupt receives "the reward of a hundred
martyrs," and Nursî recalls Imam Rabbani's testimony that the brightest of all
the saints were those who made the Sunna the ground of their path. Following it,
he argues, recalls the Prophet directly — and that recollection turns into an
awareness of God's presence, so that even eating, drinking and sleeping, done as
he did them, become a kind of worship. Two more personal points record the
spiritual storms of Nursî's own turning, including a stark vision of himself as a
corpse standing amid the dying of all living things.

At the centre is the verse "If you love God, follow me, and God will love you"
(3:31), which he reads as a tight chain of reasoning: to love God is to want to
act as He loves; that means resembling the one He loves; and resembling him means
following him — so the road to being loved by God runs through the Sunna, which
Nursî calls man's highest goal. Against this he sets **innovation** (*bid'at*):
because the religion was already "perfected" (5:3), to discard the Sunna for
novelties quietly implies it was lacking — "all innovations are misguidance." He
is careful, though, to map the Sunna's degrees (compulsory, voluntary, laudable)
and to lighten the burden: not everyone can keep every practice, but intention,
inclination and support for it still count, and even keeping the Prophet's daily
manners turns habit into worship. A final point traces the Sunna to its sources —
his words, his acts, his conduct.

Read whole, the Flash is both an argument and an invitation; in Nursî's own words
it carries a warmth a summary can only point to. The full text is linked above.
