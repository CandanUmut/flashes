The Twelfth Flash answers two questions put by a student, Re'fet Bey, and turns
both into points about reading the Qur'an well.

The **first** is about provision (*rızık*). Scripture guarantees that God feeds
every living thing, yet creatures plainly die of hunger — how can both be true?
Nursî's resolution: the guarantee holds, because the body is given a built-in
reserve (he notes it lasts roughly forty days, sometimes far longer), so death
from genuine lack of food is rare; what usually kills is broken habit or
illness, not an empty table. He adds a striking observation — provision runs in
*inverse* proportion to a creature's power and will: the unborn child and the
nursing infant, utterly helpless, are fed most freely and gently, while the
strong must go and seek their bread. From this he reads the reach of the divine
name **Provider** (*Rezzâk*), and treats the long fasts reported of certain
saints as falling within ordinary "customary possibility," not impossibility.

The **second** question defends the Qur'an's language of "seven heavens and
seven earths" against the science of Nursî's day. He first notes that the verse
does not actually say the earth has seven *layers*, only that it is *like* the
heavens; then he gathers several genuinely sevenfold ways the earth can be read,
and argues that its hidden depths, like the heavens, are surely inhabited. For
the heavens he charts a middle course — between an ancient philosophy that
imagined nine spheres and a modern science that denied the heavens altogether —
building his case (in the terms of his time, including the then-current notion
of "ether") that a single substance can be worked into seven ordered levels.

Underneath both answers lies the Flash's real lesson, which is about
interpretation: because the Qur'an addresses every level of reader at once, a
phrase like "seven heavens" carries several true meanings layered together, and
if even one is confirmed the verse stands. A science that fixes on a single
literal image and then attacks the verse, he says, is "throwing stones at a
star." The original carries the argument with far more colour; the full text is
linked above.
