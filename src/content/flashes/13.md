The Thirteenth Flash unfolds, in thirteen "Indications," the short prayer every
believer knows — "I seek refuge with God from Satan the accursed" — and uses it
to face the hard questions evil raises.

The first is the one a thoughtful person actually feels: if truth is beautiful
and God takes the side of the people of truth, why does the party of evil so
often seem to win? Nursî's answer turns on a homely fact — **destruction is
easy**. Good is constructive and positive; it takes twenty people twenty days to
raise a building one person can pull down in a day. So a weak, negative force can
inflict real damage, and the people of misguidance sometimes overcome the strong
people of truth with very little — yet the harm is temporary, "and the end is for
the righteous," and the loss is repaid many times over. A second question is
graver still: why create Satan at all, if many will be ruined through him? Here
Nursî argues that without a contest there can be no ascent. It is precisely the
struggle against evil that lets a human being develop, through effort and choice,
into something higher than the angels — just as refusing that struggle lets him
sink lower than any animal. The existence of evil is the price of a creature who
can *rise*.

From there the Flash turns practical. Belief, the Shari'a and the Prophet's
Sunna form a stronghold so secure that, once a person takes refuge inside it, the
enemy cannot reach him. And much of Satan's work, Nursî shows, is mere
**whispering** (*vesvese*) — the involuntary suggestion that flits through the
mind and which the anxious mistake for their own belief. To recognise the
whisper as *not one's own*, and simply to deny it attention, is already to defeat
it. In Nursî's own words the Indications are by turns consoling and sharply
argued; the full text is linked above.
