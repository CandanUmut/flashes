The Fourteenth Flash falls into two very different stations. The **First
Station** answers two questions. The first is about an old saying that "the earth
rests on a bull and a fish." Rather than defend or dismiss it, Nursî uses it to
teach how Hadith should be read: some strange material is borrowed Israelite
lore, and many vivid comparisons, passing from scholars to ordinary people,
slowly harden into literal tales — as the astronomers' metaphor of a "serpent"
for the lunar nodes became, in village speech, a snake that swallows the moon. He
then offers three readings of the bull and the fish — two angels who oversee the
land (farmed by oxen) and the sea (full of fish); the way human life truly
"rests" on the ox and the fish; and the constellations Taurus and Pisces, in
which he finds a veiled hint that it is the *earth* that moves. The second
question concerns the **People of the Cloak**: why the Prophet drew Ali, Fatima,
Hasan and Husayn under his mantle. Foreseeing the strife to come, Nursî says, he
meant to clear Ali in the community's eyes, console Husayn, honour Hasan's future
peacemaking, and mark Fatima's line as blessed. (As in the Fourth Flash, the
sensitive question of the early Caliphate is handled as Nursî's reading, not a
verdict.)

The **Second Station** is the beloved treatise on the **Bismillah** — "In the
Name of God, the Merciful, the Compassionate" (*besmele*) — drawing out six of
its "thousands of mysteries," and aimed, Nursî says, at the heart more than the
reason. The phrase's three parts answer to three "stamps": of Godhead on the
whole universe, of mercy (*rahmet*) on the living earth, of tender
compassion on the nature of man. Because endless multiplicity can overwhelm the
mind, the Bismillah shows the One present whole within each single thing — divine
oneness (*ehadiyet*) within divine unity — so that anyone, at any level, can turn
and say "You alone do we worship." Above all it is the doorway to mercy: it is
mercy, Nursî argues, that turns the entire universe toward weak and needy man,
and the Bismillah is how a person takes hold of it — found, finally, by following
the Prophet, the "Mercy to all the Worlds."

The Bismillah treatise in particular is among the collection's most loved pages,
and reads with a warmth a summary can only point at; the full text is linked
above.
