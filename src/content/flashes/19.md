The Nineteenth Flash, built on the verse "eat and drink, but waste not by excess"
(7:31), is a treatise in seven points on **frugality** (*iktisâd*) and
contentment — and against waste (*isrâf*).

Its first move reframes thrift as gratitude. Because the All-Compassionate gives
His gifts wanting **thanks** (*şükür*) in return, frugality — which honours the
gift and uses it with care — is itself a kind of thanks, and even, Nursî says, a
cause of plenty; waste, by contrast, slights the bounty and ends in loss. He then
offers the Flash's governing image: the **sense of taste is a doorkeeper**, not
the master of the body. The stomach is the ruler; the palate deserves only a
small "tip" for its service. Pamper it — pay it nine times its due for half a
minute's pleasure on the tongue — and it forgets its post, throws open the gates
to every craving, and stages a revolt that ends in indigestion and illness. For
the grateful and reflective, though, that same sense of taste can be promoted: it
becomes "an inspector in the kitchens of divine mercy," weighing the varieties of
God's bounty and reporting them back as thanks.

From there the Flash draws out the wider fruits of frugality: it keeps the body
healthy and the self in dignity, and — crucially — it frees a person from the
quiet degradation of dependence and begging. Extravagance manufactures a false,
restless appetite that can never be satisfied and so breeds endless need; the
spendthrift, for all his spending, grows poor, while the frugal person, content
with little, is in truth the rich one. Provision (*rızık*) received with thanks
and measure becomes worship; squandered, it becomes a master over the soul
(*nefis*).

In Nursî's own words the argument is brisk, witty and concrete; the full text is
