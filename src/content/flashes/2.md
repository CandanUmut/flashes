The Second Flash takes as its starting point a single verse: the cry of the
Prophet Job (*Eyyüb*) from the depths of his illness — "affliction has touched
me, and You are the most merciful of the merciful" (21:83). Nursî retells the
story with a precise emphasis. Job bore his bodily wounds patiently for the sake
of their reward, and prayed for healing only when the affliction reached his
heart and tongue — the seats of remembering and worshipping God — and so
threatened his worship. He asked, in other words, not for his own comfort but
for the sake of his service. That distinction sets the tone for everything that
follows.

From there the treatise unfolds in **five points**. The **first** turns the
story inward: our sins and doubts are wounds too, and because they imperil our
endless life rather than a brief earthly one, they are graver than Job's. Nursî
traces, soberingly, how a single unrepented sin can grow until a person
half-wishes that the truths it offends — the recording angels, Hell, the duty of
worship, even God — simply did not exist.

The **middle points** build a practical theology of hardship. Illness and
misfortune are not grounds for complaint: the body is a canvas for God's names
(the *Healer* implies that illness exists; the *Provider*, that hunger does);
trials refine and strengthen a life that pure ease would leave stagnant; and
this world is a place of service, not of reward, so suffering patiently borne
itself counts as worship — what Nursî calls *negative worship*, the undiluted
turning-to-God of one who feels his own helplessness. He adds a sharp insight
about patience (*sabır*): God grants enough for each day, but we squander it
grieving a past whose pain is already spent and dreading a future not yet
arrived, leaving none for the hour we are actually in.

The **fifth point** separates real misfortune — what harms one's religion — from
the apparent kind, which may be a warning, a purification, or even a hidden
favour; and it counsels meeting trouble with contentment (*rıza*) and trust
(*tevekkül*) rather than the anxiety that, Nursî says, drives an ailment from the
body into the heart. A closing meditation casts the human being as a "factory of
thanks" and a pen inscribing the divine names, set in motion as much by pain as
by ease.

Read in Nursî's own words, the argument carries a consolation and a force no
summary can hold; the full text is linked above.
