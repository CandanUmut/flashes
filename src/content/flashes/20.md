The Twentieth Flash is the close companion of the better-known Twenty-First, and
it opens not with a definition of **sincerity** (*ihlâs*) but with a wound. Built
on the verse "worship God in sincerity" (39:2) and a sobering Hadith — that all
will perish save the scholars, and the scholars save those who act on their
knowledge, and these save the sincere, "and even the sincere are in great
danger" — it asks an awful question: why is it that the worldly, the neglectful,
even the misguided, manage to cooperate, while the people of religion — the
scholars and the followers of the Sufi path, who are supposed to be the people of
concord — so often fall into rivalry? Agreement and dispute, he says, seem to
have changed places.

His answer unfolds through seven causes, and they cut close. The worldly, he
observes, have their functions and rewards fixed and parcelled out, so there is
nothing held in common to fight over; the people of truth, by contrast, all reach
toward the *same* undivided prize — the regard, esteem and attention of others —
and so their hands collide. Their very cooperation among the worldly springs from
weakness and need, while the discord among the people of guidance springs,
perversely, from a misplaced sense of dignity. Beneath it all lies the craving to
be noticed — the love of station (*hubb-u câh*) that is the seed of showing off
(*riya*) and the enemy of brotherhood (*uhuvvet*).

The cure is sincerity, and Nursî states it plainly: prefer the worship of God to
the worship of one's own soul; let God's pleasure outweigh the ego's
(*enaniyet*); renounce both the material and the moral reward to be had from
people, holding with the prophets that "my reward is from God alone" (11:29); and
treat men's acceptance not as something to be chased but as God's affair entirely
— even preferring others to oneself (59:9). In Nursî's own words the diagnosis is
unsparing and the remedy bracing; the full text is linked above.
