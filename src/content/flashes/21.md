The Twenty-First Flash, widely known as the **Treatise on Sincerity (İhlâs
Risâlesi)**, takes up a single question: how to make one's work purely for God.
Nursî places sincerity (*ihlâs*) — acting for God's pleasure alone — at the very
foundation of anything done for the hereafter, arguing that the worth of a deed
turns far more on the purity behind it than on its size or visible success. So
weighty is the matter that he asked his readers to return to this short treatise
at least once **every fifteen days**.

The Flash first names what *destroys* sincerity. Three forces, in particular,
quietly corrupt it: **rivalry** that grows out of competing for material gain;
the **love of position and fame** (*hubb-u câh*), which he treats as a hidden
form of associating partners with God — the root of showing off (*riya*); and
plain **fear and greed**. Each turns the eye away from God and toward the self
and the crowd.

Against these he sets **four rules** for gaining and guarding sincerity: to seek
only God's pleasure in the work itself; not to criticise fellow workers or to
parade one's own virtue and so stir up envy; to recognise that all genuine
strength lies in truth and sincerity rather than in numbers or cleverness; and
to take a grateful pride in the merits of one's brothers as if they were one's
own — the self-effacing fellowship he calls *tefânî*.

Two further supports protect the whole: keeping one's **death** consciously in
mind (*râbıta-i mevt*), which loosens the long-stretched hopes that feed
ambition, and the constant awareness of standing in **God's presence**. Finally,
through the famous images of the **lamp** and the **needle**, Nursî describes the
secret of **shared reward** (*iştirâk-i a'mâl*): where sincerity and unity are
real, the light of the whole community's worship is, by God's grace, reflected
in each member's account.
