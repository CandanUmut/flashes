The Twenty-Second Flash is a short, confidential piece Nursî wrote in his Barla
exile for his closest students, and then — pointedly — dedicated to the very
authorities who were watching him, to show that "my most hidden secret is this
treatise." It answers, in three "Indications," the pressure he was living under.

The **first** meets a natural question: if he has withdrawn from the world, why
do the authorities keep interfering with him? Nursî declines to plead his own
case and hands the question to the people and officials of Isparta — who, having
gained strength of belief through the Risale over his nine years there, have more
reason and standing to answer for him than he does himself; a man with thousands
of advocates need not argue his own defence. The **second** answers the charge
that, by holding a position of respect outside the state, he offends the new
creed of "absolute equality." Such enforced equality, he argues, runs against
human nature and the wisdom in man's creation: a single human being was made
comprehensive enough to stand for a thousand species, and the engine of that
richness is the **virtue that grows from belief** — which is the opposite of
tyranny, since its people exert influence only through humility, poverty and
their own powerlessness.

The **third** Indication is the heart of it. To the complaint that he accepts
respect without holding any office, he replies that a human being is not only a
body bound for the grave: the gravest duty of all is to hand a traveller "a
passport" and "a light" for the road beyond death — and that is what teaching
belief is. The dignity he assumes, he insists, is borrowed for the honour of the
Qur'an, not claimed for himself; he wants the people's prayers, not their
applause. He closes with a striking confession: whenever "the worldly" wounded
him unjustly, he would examine his own soul and find there some hidden flicker of
ego (*enaniyet*) or self-display — so that their injustice, under **divine
determining** (*kader*), turned out to be the very thing that scoured his
**sincerity** (*ihlâs*) clean.

In Nursî's own voice the piece is by turns defiant and disarmingly humble; the
full text is linked above.
