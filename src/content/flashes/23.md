The Twenty-Third Flash, known as the **Treatise on Nature** (*Tabiat Risâlesi*),
is Nursî's frontal answer to materialist atheism — and he says plainly that its
tone is sharper than the Risale's usual mildness, because it was written against
attacks on belief he met in Ankara in 1922. Its method is disarmingly simple. A
thing that exists, he says, can be accounted for in only four ways: **its causes
made it; it made itself; "nature" made it; or the One All-Powerful made it.** If
the first three can be shown to be impossible, the fourth — divine unity
(*tevhîd*) — stands proven by elimination.

So he takes them in turn, each demolished by what he calls "impossibilities."
*"Causes create it"* (*esbâb*): but a living body is compounded in exact
measure from countless different substances — as absurd to credit to blind causes
as to imagine a healing potion mixing itself when a pharmacy's jars are knocked
over; and a single, perfectly balanced living unity can only issue from a single
hand. *"It forms itself"*: then each tiny particle in your body would need an
eye that sees the whole organism and the intelligence of a thousand Platos; your
body is a thousand-domed palace whose stones hold themselves in order — it is
*written* by one pen, not *printed* by a machine that would itself require
infinite machines. *"Nature makes it"* (*tabiat*): but as no real sun can dwell
inside each fragment of glass that reflects it, no blind nature can contain the
knowledge, will and power to fashion one living thing — that would be to hide a
god in every speck of soil.

What, then, is "nature"? At most, Nursî answers, it is God's **artwork, not the
Artist** — a decree, not the Decree-giver; a law, not a power; a created screen to
the divine dignity, behind which apparent faults can be ascribed to causes while
God's power works directly. He then lets a convinced "convert" voice the lingering
doubts and answers them: why God tolerates no partner in creation (a ruler by
nature rejects all interference); why worship is owed at all (you are the patient,
and worship is the remedy); the wonderful **ease** of creation through oneness
(commanding a hundred soldiers through one officer is a hundred times easier than
one soldier through a hundred) — the secret of *ehadiyet*; and why "nothing comes
from nothing" is itself the confused creed of those who cannot bear the
difficulty their own system creates.

This is among the most rigorous and beloved pieces in the collection, and in
Nursî's own words its force is considerable; the full text is linked above.
