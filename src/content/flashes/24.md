The Twenty-Fourth Flash, known as the *Tesettür Risâlesi*, takes up the verse in
which the Prophet is told to have the believing women "bring down over themselves
[part] of their outer garments" (33:59), and defends the practice of covering
against the secularising culture of Nursî's own time and place, which — he
writes — called it unnatural and "a kind of slavery." It is among the more
sensitive pieces in the collection; what follows is a description of *his*
argument, set out as he makes it, not a ruling on a contested question.

Nursî answers with what he calls four "instances of wisdom," and his recurring
move is to claim that the injunction is not imposed *against* a woman's nature but
*expresses* it. In his telling: a woman's innate disposition (he leans heavily on
*şefkat*, tenderness, and on a natural sense of modesty) inclines her to wish to
be protected and esteemed rather than exposed to every passing gaze; covering, on
this view, guards that disposition and with it the warmth, trust and security of
family life; it shields a woman from being reduced to an object of appetite; and
so, he concludes, it is the *un*covering urged by "dissolute civilisation," not
the veil, that runs against women's deeper interests and instincts.

A reader will recognise that this is a particular argument, shaped by a
particular moment, and that the questions it touches are debated within and beyond
the Muslim community to this day. The companion's task here is only to convey
faithfully what the Flash says and to send you to Nursî's own words, where the
case is made at length and in its own register; the full text is linked above.
