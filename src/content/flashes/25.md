The Twenty-Fifth Flash is a "Message for the Sick" — twenty-five short
**remedies** Nursî wrote, by his own account quickly and as a single rough draft,
to be read at a sickbed like a prescription. Each is addressed straight to the
patient ("Unhappy sick person…"), and together they work to turn a person's whole
relation to illness around.

The opening remedies set the key. Sickness, Nursî argues, is "not a malady but a
sort of cure": a life is capital, and if it slips by in ease and heedlessness it
is squandered, whereas illness slows that squander and makes the capital *yield*.
Borne with **patience** (*sabır*), it even becomes a kind of worship — what he
calls "negative worship," in which the helpless soul, feeling its own weakness,
turns and clings to its Compassionate Creator so purely that no hypocrisy can
enter; on this footing a single patient minute can carry the weight of an hour's
prayer. A further remedy casts illness as the most honest of advisers: good
health quietly persuades us we are immortal and lets the grave be forgotten,
while sickness "opens the eyes" and says, *you are not your own; prepare.* And
because the body and its faculties were never our property to begin with but a
trust from their Owner, the patient has, strictly, no ground for complaint — only
for thanks (*şükür*) and contentment (*rıza*); to dwell on a pain already spent
is merely to suffer it twice.

The remaining remedies fan out with real tenderness (*şefkat*) to meet particular
sufferers — the bedridden, those who have lost sight or strength, the lonely, the
dying — and each, in its own way, lifts the eye from the wound to the One who
sends and can heal it. Written at speed and from the heart, the original has a
directness no summary holds; the full text is linked above.
