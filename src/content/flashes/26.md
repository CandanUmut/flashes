The Twenty-Sixth Flash, the "Treatise for the Elderly," is one of the most
personal and beloved pieces in the collection. It is built of twenty-six
**Hopes**, and each has the same shape: Nursî first describes, with disarming
honesty, a real sorrow from his own later life — the discovery of his own age,
the loneliness of exile, separation from those he loved, the approach of death —
and then sets beside it the "light" he found in belief, so that the reader can
see how directly the Qur'an's remedies answer a grief they have just felt.

He opens, fittingly, with the aged prophet Zakariya's prayer — "my bones have
weakened, and my head has filled with white" (19:4) — and the Hopes that follow
return again and again to a single discovery. In one, gazing from a high mountain
at an autumn dusk, he feels the self, the day, the year and the whole world
growing old together, and a dark sorrow overwhelms him — until he sees that the
cure is not to cling to the fading world but to turn to the Eternal One it points
to. In another, the grave changes its face entirely: for one who believes, it is
not a pit of extinction but a door opening on a world where those who went ahead
are waiting, so that age, far from being only loss, is a drawing-*nearer* to that
reunion. And running through them all is a quiet reversal — that the very
**weakness** of old age, like a child's helplessness, is what most powerfully
turns a person toward the All-Merciful, until reliance (*tevekkül*) and
contentment (*rıza*) become the old believer's real strength, and **belief**
(*îmân*) itself the spring from which every Hope flows.

Because each Hope begins in genuine pain, the consolation lands with unusual
weight; in Nursî's own words it has comforted many, and a summary can only point
toward it. The full text is linked above.
