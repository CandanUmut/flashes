The Twenty-Eighth Flash is not a single treatise but a sheaf of short pieces
Nursî wrote in Eskişehir Prison in 1935, as consolation for the brothers held in
the ward opposite his — at a time when he was forbidden to mix or even speak with
them. Read together, they show a particular gift: the ability to turn a cell into
a window.

It opens, characteristically, with something tiny. Autumn has come, the flies are
near the end of their season, and the prisoners are using chemicals to be rid of
them — which stirs in Nursî "a sharp pity." Watching the flies line up "in most
orderly fashion," like miniature birds, on his washing-line, he draws from them a
point about a verse and about the dignity God lends even the least of His
creatures. From there the pieces range widely: brief meditations on the element
of air, on the divine names, on points of Qur'anic eloquence; fragments of the
**defence** he made before the court; and a run of short counsels for the inner
life under pressure — on the evil-commanding **soul** (*nefis*), on "the times of
sleep," on what to do at festival times when one is cut off from family.

What holds the miscellany together is its setting and its purpose. These are the
notes of a man keeping his own and his friends' hearts awake in confinement,
practising the contemplation (*tefekkür*) that finds the order and mercy of
creation legible even from behind bars, and steadying belief (*îmân*) where it is
most tested. Because the pieces are occasional and personal, they reward reading
in Nursî's own words far more than any summary; the full text is linked above.
