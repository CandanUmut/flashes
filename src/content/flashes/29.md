The Twenty-Ninth Flash is a piece of pure contemplation. Originally written in
Arabic, what stands here is its **Third Chapter** — "On the Degrees of 'God is
Most Great'" — which Nursî set down in prison, at his brothers' insistence,
because he feared the long sequences of reflective thought (*tefekkür*) he had
carried for years might otherwise be lost. (The remaining chapters were published
elsewhere.)

Its structure is itself a kind of prayer. The phrase *Allāhu akbar* — "God is
greater," greater than anything the mind can frame — is unfolded through
thirty-three "degrees," of which this chapter climbs seven. Each degree is a rung:
the contemplative gaze settles on one rank of creation, reads in its order, its
beauty and its utter dependence a witness that its Maker is beyond it, and so
arrives once more at the same declaration of greatness — then steps up to the next
rank and hears it again, louder. The ascent runs from the smallest particulars
toward the whole cosmos, and what changes along the way is not the testimony but
its depth: the same God is found wholly present in each single thing and sovereign
over all of them at once — the **oneness within unity** (*ehadiyet*) that is
Nursî's recurring note, here turned into a ladder of **knowledge of God**
(*marifetullah*).

Because the Flash is built to be *done* rather than merely read — a contemplation
to climb, not an argument to follow — its life is in Nursî's own words far more
than in any account of it. The full text is linked above.
