The Third Flash is a meditation on the verse "Everything will perish but His
Face" (28:88) and on the invocation that distils it — "O Eternal One, You alone
are Eternal" (*Yâ Bâkî Ente'l-Bâkî*). Nursî warns at the outset that the piece
runs on feeling more than argument, and should not be measured on the scales of
logic.

Its **First Point** turns on a single image: said once, the invocation works
"like a surgical operation," cutting the heart free of everything that passes.
A human being, Nursî observes, is wired to love without limit; but poured out on
transient things, that love becomes a source of endless pain, because the
beloved always departs. So one learns to leave them before they leave —
reserving the heart's deepest love for the One who remains. Said a second time,
the same words become a *salve*: since the Eternal exists and endures, He
suffices and stands in for all that is lost; the beauty we loved in creatures was
only a faint shadow of the manifestation of His **most beautiful names**
(*esmâ-i hüsnâ*).

The **Second Point** takes up the human craving for permanence — so deep that we
imagine a kind of immortality in everything we love, and weep at every parting.
That very longing, Nursî suggests, is no accident but a sign: a universal, innate
prayer that the All-Generous answered by creating an eternal realm for a
perishing creature. The **Third Point** then distinguishes the "spheres" of
body, heart and spirit, which live in ever-widening reaches of time, and presses
a startling claim: a single second spent in the knowledge (*marifetullah*) and
love of the Truly Enduring One can outweigh years, while a heedless (*gaflet*)
lifetime passes like a moment. He gathers the signs of this "expansion of time"
— the Night of Power worth a thousand months, the Prophet's Ascension, the
experiences of the saints, even the long dramas packed into a minute's dream.

In short: transient though he is, the human being was made to mirror the Eternal
and to bear fruit that lasts; his happiness is to cling, with every faculty, to
the Eternal One's names within the bounds of His pleasure. The original carries a
warmth and lift no summary reproduces; the full text is linked above.
