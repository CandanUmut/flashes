The Thirtieth Flash is the longest in the collection and one of its summits: six
"Points," each devoted to one of God's **greatest names** (*esmâ-i hüsnâ*),
reading that name off the face of the universe so that creation itself becomes a
single legible witness. Several Points were written in Eskişehir Prison and, Nursî
warns, left in a quick, uncorrected state.

He begins with **the Most Holy** (*Kuddûs*). The universe, he observes, is a vast
factory in ceaseless operation and the earth a guesthouse endlessly filled and
emptied — and yet, unlike any human workshop, it stays spotless: decay, waste and
death are swept up and recycled almost as fast as they appear, the whole kept
fresh and clean. That perfect, tireless cleansing he reads, "like the sun," as
the signature of the All-Holy. The **All-Just** (*Adl*) is read next from the
measured balance that holds the cosmos and every creature in exact proportion;
**the Sapient** (*Hakem*) from the wisdom fitted into every "word, letter and
point" of the book of the universe, each so purposeful that all causes together
could not fashion one of them. The **Single** (*Ferd*) — a manifestation Nursî
records coming to him in prison — is the stamp of uniqueness running through both
the whole and each part, so that the unity of all things points unmistakably to
the One.

The treatise rises toward life: **the Ever-Living** (*Hayy*), since life is the
universe's "most important result, greatest aim and most valuable fruit," is its
brightest sign; and **the Self-Subsistent** (*Kayyûm*) is read from the plain
fact that nothing in creation stands by itself — all things subsist, instant by
instant, only through Him. Taken together the six Points are a sustained exercise
in **knowledge of God** (*marifetullah*): not abstract argument but a way of
*seeing*, in which order, balance, wisdom, uniqueness, life and dependence all
say one thing. Each Point can be read on its own, and in Nursî's own words their
force is considerable; the full text is linked above.
