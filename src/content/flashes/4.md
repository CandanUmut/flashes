The Fourth Flash is built on three verses: the Prophet's aching concern for his
community (9:128), his charge to rely on God if people turn away (9:129), and his
being told to ask "no reward but love of close kin" (42:23). What survives here
is its First Station, in four points; the Second Station was later set apart as
the Eleventh Flash.

The **first two points** dwell on the Prophet's compassion. Sound reports, Nursî
notes, have him crying "My community! My community!" amid the terror of the
Resurrection while others think only of themselves. His unusual tenderness toward
his grandsons **Hasan and Husayn**, Nursî reads, was not mere family affection
but a glance ahead: each was the "index" of a line of luminous guides — figures
like ʿAbd al-Qādir al-Gīlānī — who would inherit and uphold the Prophet's way.
The **third point** explains the love the verse asks for his **Family** (the
*Âl-i Beyt*): from it would spring many of Islam's teachers, and the saying "I
leave you two things — God's Book and my Family" means, for Nursî, holding fast
to the Qur'an and to the Prophet's practice (*sünnet*), which his Family were
charged to guard.

The **fourth point** is the delicate one. Here Nursî takes up the long dispute
over whether Abū Bakr or ʿAlī was the most worthy of the early Caliphate. He
asks the reader to consider ʿAlī in two respects: his personal perfections, and
his role as representative of the Family's **collective personality** (*şahs-ı
mânevî*). On the first, he argues — stating the Sunni position — that the service
of the first Caliphs gave them precedence; on the second, that as bearer of the
Family's legacy ʿAlī "has no equal," and he honours him as the "King of
Sainthood." He then distinguishes a "Sainthood Shiʿa," whose excess of love for
ʿAlī he treats as an excusable devotion, from a politically-driven "Caliphate
Shiʿa," whom he faults for partisanship. (This is Nursî's own framing of a
contested history, offered as his reading rather than a verdict.)

He closes with the Flash's real heart: an impassioned appeal to Sunnis and
Alevis to abandon a quarrel that, he warns, only arms those hostile to faith to
turn one against the other — for a hundred sacred bonds already bind them in
brotherhood (*uhuvvet*). The original argues the point at length and with care;
the full text is linked above.
