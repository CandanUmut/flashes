The Seventh Flash reads the three verses that close Sūrat al-Fath (48:27–29) as
a sample of one of the ten "aspects" of the Qur'an's miraculous inimitability
(*i'câz*): its **tidings of the Unseen** (*ihbâr-ı gaybî*) — its foretelling of
things hidden when it was revealed. From these verses Nursî draws out seven such
predictions, all, he argues, borne out by events.

The first four are historical: the conquest of Mecca, which came two years later;
the Truce of Hudaybiya, outwardly a setback but in truth a "manifest victory"
that opened hearts (so that fierce opponents like Khālid and ʿAmr soon entered
Islam); the promise of a safe, fearless circumambulation of the Ka'ba; and the
triumph of the new faith over the great religions and empires of the age. The
next, read allusively, finds the distinguishing traits of the four
**Rightly-Guided Caliphs** folded into the verse's phrases — "strong against the
unbelievers," "merciful among themselves," "bowing and prostrating." Two further
predictions concern the **Companions** (*Sahâbe*): their likeness foretold "in
the Torah" and "in the Gospel," pictured as a seed that sends up its shoot, grows
thick and firm, and enrages those who oppose it.

A **Postscript** extends the method to another verse (4:68–69). Its roll-call —
"the Prophets, the Veracious, the Witnesses, and the Righteous" — Nursî reads as
pointing, beneath its plain sense, to Abū Bakr (the Veracious), to the three
later Caliphs who died as martyrs (the Witnesses), and even, by a turn of
phrasing, to Hasan as a fifth Caliph.

Underneath the particulars lies the Flash's real claim, which is about method:
because the Qur'an proceeds from an all-encompassing knowledge, its words carry
many layered meanings at once, and this single strand of its miraculousness — its
veiled news of the Unseen — runs not to the forty or fifty verses literalists
allow but into the thousands. The original sets out each prediction with its
evidences; the full text is linked above.
