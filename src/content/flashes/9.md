The Ninth Flash is a letter to a student, and Nursî opens it with an unusual
warning: not everyone should read it, because not everyone will see the subtle
errors it discusses, or has need of them. The subject is the Sufi doctrine of
the **Unity of Existence** (*vahdetü'l-vücud*) — the view, associated with the
great mystic Muhyiddin Ibn al-ʿArabi, that in the end "there is no existent but
God," so that the existence of created things is in some sense imaginary.

What is striking is the respect with which Nursî disagrees. Ibn al-ʿArabi, he
insists, was a true saint whose unveilings were real; "he would not deceive, but
he could be deceived" — what he saw was correct, but he mistook it for the whole
of reality, and, having chosen an independent path, strained some Qur'anic
verses to fit his observations. To mark the difference, Nursî offers his
**mirror-and-sun** image: the sun reflected in a mirror is a genuine
manifestation (*tecellî*) of the sun, but it is not the sun. In the same way,
each created thing is a mirror to the divine names — its beauty real, yet only a
shadow of theirs. So the truth, he says, is "**all from Him**," not "all Him":
things do have an existence, weak and wholly dependent, sustained moment by
moment by their Creator — and to deny it is to blur a verse like "the reality of
things is constant."

The letter answers two further questions along the way: a request to be taught
the lettrist science of *jafr*, which Nursî gently declines as a distraction
from the far weightier service of teaching the truths of belief; and a claim
that Jesus had a human father, which he refutes from the verse likening Jesus's
creation to Adam's. He then closes by distinguishing two roots of the Unity of
Existence — an intellect that cannot contain the full breadth of God's direct
creativity, and a love so afraid of separation that it dissolves the beloved
into the One — and sets above it the sober **Unity of Witnessing**
(*vahdetü'ş-şuhud*), which keeps every divine name real and the balance of
belief intact.

This is one of the subtler pieces in the collection, and Nursî's own caution is
worth heeding; the full text is linked above.
